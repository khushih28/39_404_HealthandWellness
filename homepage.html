What is Dyslexia?

Dyslexia is a learning disorder characterized by difficulties in reading, spelling, and writing, despite normal intelligence and educational opportunities. It is a neurological condition that affects the way the brain processes written and spoken language. Dyslexia is not related to vision problems but rather how the brain interprets symbols and sounds.With the right support, including tailored teaching methods, assistive technologies, and a positive learning environment, people with dyslexia can achieve their full potential and thrive in various domains of life.

Early Detection Dyslexia

Early Action

Early detection of dyslexia is essential for improving a child’s academic, social, and emotional well-being. When identified early, children can receive targeted interventions and specialized instruction tailored to their unique learning needs. This helps them develop foundational literacy skills such as decoding, reading fluency, and spelling, which are critical for academic success. Early intervention also prevents the widening of educational gaps, ensuring that children with dyslexia can keep pace with their peers. Beyond academics, early detection fosters confidence and self-esteem by reducing the frustration and feelings of inadequacy that often accompany undiagnosed learning difficulties. It allows children to focus on their strengths while receiving the support they need to overcome challenges, setting them up for long-term success both in and out of the classroom.

Take a Test

1.What is the age Group of the person?

2. Does the person have trouble reading smoothly and often guess or skip words?

3. Is spelling difficult, with mistakes like mixing up or leaving out letters?

4. Do they read much slower than others their age?

5. Is it hard for them to match letters with their sounds (like "b" with the "buh" sound)?

6. Do they forget things in order, like the alphabet or days of the week?

7. Do they confuse directions (like left and right) or mix up similar letters (like "b" and "d")?

8. Do they avoid reading or writing because it feels too hard?

9. Do they say words wrong often or struggle with rhyming?

10. Is there someone else in the family with learning or reading problems?

11. Do they get upset or lose confidence when it comes to reading or writing?
